Go Screening Kit · Part 4 · 3 questions · pick 1–2 per screen

Data & infrastructure

A backend service is mostly a polite wrapper around a database. These questions check whether they understand the machinery under database/sql and — via the incident question — whether they've ever been the one holding the pager.

Question 14

"How does Go manage database connections? What do SetMaxOpenConns, SetMaxIdleConns, and SetConnMaxLifetime actually control, and what happens when the pool is exhausted?"

Why ask: pool exhaustion is one of the most common Go-service production incidents, and the settings are invisible until it happens to you.

Answer guideline — a strong answer covers

  • *sql.DB is not a connection — it's a pool, safe for concurrent use, opened once and shared (not per-request).
  • SetMaxOpenConns: hard cap on concurrent connections (0 = unlimited — dangerous, the DB has its own limit). SetMaxIdleConns: how many to keep warm. SetConnMaxLifetime: recycle connections, which matters behind LBs/proxies and for failover.
  • Exhaustion behavior: the next query blocks waiting for a free connection — the service doesn't error, it slows down, latency climbs, and with no context deadline it can hang. Symptoms: latency spikes with low CPU.
  • Bonus: leaking connections by not closing rows (defer rows.Close()); sizing against the DB's max_connections across all replicas; db.Stats() / metrics for pool wait counts.

Strong signals

  • "It's a pool, queries block when it's empty" — the core model
  • Has a pool-exhaustion or rows.Close()-leak story
  • Thinks about total connections across instances vs DB limits

Weak / red flags

  • Opens a connection per request, or thinks sql.Open connects at all (it's lazy — Ping to verify)
  • Never touched the pool settings in 3–4 years ("defaults were fine" without knowing what the defaults are)
  • Can't describe any symptom of exhaustion

3/5 = pool model + what the knobs do. 5/5 = exhaustion symptoms and a real tuning/leak story. ORM users (GORM etc.) should still know this — it's the same pool underneath.

Question 15

"Your service layer needs to call two repository methods atomically — same transaction. How do you structure that without leaking *sql.Tx everywhere?"

Why ask: a design problem with no single right answer — you get to watch them weigh trade-offs, which is the most senior-shaped signal a screen can produce.

Answer guideline — acceptable shapes (any one, argued well)

  • Explicit tx passing: repo methods take a queryer interface satisfied by both *sql.DB and *sql.Tx; the service opens the tx and passes it down. Verbose but honest.
  • Tx-in-context or a WithinTx(ctx, fn) helper: a transactor wraps fn in begin/commit/rollback; repos pull the tx from ctx if present. Cleaner call sites, slightly magical.
  • Unit-of-work / tx-scoped repo factory: tx.UserRepo().Save(...). More machinery, clearest boundaries.

Regardless of shape, must-mention hygiene

  • defer rollback pattern so errors and panics can't leave a transaction open; commit as the last step.
  • Keep transactions short — no HTTP calls or heavy computation inside; long transactions hold locks and starve the pool (ties back to Q14).
  • Transaction logic belongs to the service layer (it owns the business invariant), not inside a single repo.

Strong signals

  • Presents one shape and names its trade-off vs another
  • Rollback-on-error/panic hygiene stated without prompting
  • Knows why long transactions are dangerous (locks + pool)

Weak / red flags

  • "The ORM handles transactions" and can't go one level deeper
  • Would call two repos and hope ("it usually works")
  • Puts a network call inside the transaction without hesitation

3/5 = one working shape + rollback hygiene. 5/5 = compares shapes, owns the trade-off, short-transaction discipline.

Question 16

"Tell me about a performance problem you personally debugged in production. What was the symptom, how did you narrow it down, and what was the actual root cause?"

Why ask: the single best experience-verification question in the whole kit. Invented stories collapse under two follow-ups; real ones get more specific.

Answer guideline — what a real story sounds like

  • A concrete symptom with numbers: "p99 went from 80ms to 2s after the Tuesday deploy", "memory climbed 100MB/hour until OOM" — not "it was slow".
  • A narrowing method: metrics/dashboards → logs/traces → profiling. For a Go dev, pprof should appear here: CPU profile, heap profile, goroutine dump — or an honest "we found it via query logs before profiling was needed".
  • A specific root cause: missing index / N+1 queries, goroutine leak, unbounded cache, lock contention, connection-pool starvation, huge allocations in a hot path…
  • Verification: how they confirmed the fix (the graph came down, the profile changed) — and ideally what they changed so it can't silently regress (alert, test, dashboard).

How to probe (pick two)

  • "What did you look at first, and why?"
  • "What did pprof actually show?" — anyone who has read a flame graph can describe one; anyone who hasn't, can't.
  • "What was your wrong hypothesis along the way?" — real debugging always has one; polished stories with no dead ends are suspicious.
  • "What would you instrument differently now?"

Strong signals

  • Numbers, tool names, dead ends, and "I" doing specific actions
  • Story gets more detailed under follow-up questions
  • Ends with a prevention step, not just a fix

Weak / red flags

  • Only "we" — their personal role never materializes
  • Story gets vaguer under follow-ups
  • 3–4 years of backend work and no debugging story at all

3/5 = real story, modest tooling (logs + dashboards). 5/5 = profiling-driven diagnosis with verification. Weight this question heavily — it's the hardest to fake.
