1 · Why timed drills, not another new concept

An interview rarely asks "explain groupby." It hands you a table and a vague goal — "find the second-biggest order per customer" — and watches whether you reach for the right method within a few minutes, without re-deriving it from first principles. That's a different skill from following a lesson: it's pattern recognition, not pattern construction. The fix is the same one used for spaced repetition anywhere — solve the same shape of problem again, cold, until reaching for rank() or merge() stops requiring a conscious search.

2 · Drill 1 — the "Nth highest" pattern

One of the most common interview shapes, in SQL or pandas: "the second-highest X per group." You already built the machinery in Lesson 7 — groupby(...).rank(ascending=False) — the only new part is recognizing the shape fast: rank, then filter to the N you want. A customer with only one order simply has no rank-2 row; nothing special to handle, the filter already excludes them.

3 · Drill 2 — merge, then aggregate

Lesson 5's merge, applied to a fresh question: "revenue by region, but only for customers who actually ordered." An inner merge does the filtering for free — a customer present in customers.csv with zero rows in the cleaned orders simply produces zero merged rows, so they never reach the groupby at all. No explicit "if customer has orders" check needed; picking the right join type is the check.

4 · Drill 3 — naming the pattern you already used: anti-join

Lesson 5 found "customers with no orders" using indicator=True and _merge == "left_only", without a name for the pattern itself. It has one: an anti-join. It answers "what's missing," the mirror image of an inner join's "what matches." SQL spells the same idea as WHERE NOT EXISTS (...) or LEFT JOIN ... WHERE right.key IS NULL — worth saying by name out loud in an interview, since "I need an anti-join here" reads as more fluent than re-deriving the left-merge-then-filter trick from scratch every time.

Today's version asks for it as one chain — tying Lesson 8's chaining habit onto Lesson 5's join logic, since in practice you'd rarely leave a throwaway intermediate variable around for a check this small.

Q: "How would you find customers who've never placed an order, given an orders table and a customers table?" A strong answer, out loud: "That's an anti-join — I'd left-merge customers with orders on the customer key with indicator=True, then filter to rows where _merge is left_only, meaning that customer had no match on the orders side. In SQL I'd reach for the same idea as a LEFT JOIN with a WHERE orders.id IS NULL, or NOT EXISTS. I'd avoid a Python loop checking membership per customer — that's O(n·m) and exactly what merge is built to do in one vectorized pass." The subtext: naming "anti-join" by name, and explicitly ruling out a loop, signals fluency beyond just getting the right answer.
