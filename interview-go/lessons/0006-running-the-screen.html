Go Screening Kit · Part 6 · 2 questions + the playbook · always ask both

Calibration, red flags & running the screen

Parts 1–5 measure knowledge. This part measures experience — whether their 3–4 years were four years of growth or one year repeated four times — and gives you the structure to run the whole session.

Question 19

"What's the largest Go service you've personally owned? Rough traffic, team size — and which parts were specifically yours?"

Why ask: calibrates every other answer in the interview. A candidate who was brilliant on Q6 but has only ever owned a cron job needs different follow-ups than one who ran a 5k-rps API.

Answer guideline — what to listen for

  • "I", not "we". Let them say "we built…" once, then redirect: "and your part specifically?" A 3–4 year dev should have owned at least one service end-to-end — design through deploy through incident.
  • Numbers with error bars are fine; no numbers is not. "Maybe 200 rps at peak, 4 services, team of 5" is a real memory. Someone who genuinely operated a service knows its rough shape.
  • Ownership verbs: designed, migrated, was on-call for, debugged, deleted. "Worked on" and "was involved in" are participation verbs — probe them.
  • Scale honesty is a positive signal: "it was small, ~50 rps, but I owned all of it" beats inflated vagueness.

Strong signals

  • Concrete scope + their named slice of it
  • Mentions operating it (deploys, on-call, incidents), not just writing it
  • Comfortable saying what they did NOT do

Weak / red flags

  • Every answer stays "we" after two redirects
  • No idea of traffic, instance count, or data size — ever operated it?
  • Claims sole ownership of implausibly much (matches nothing else they said)

Not scored 1–5 — instead it sets the calibration context you'll use when weighing every technical score. Note their real scope at the top of the scorecard.

Question 20

"What's something about Go you dislike, or that has genuinely bitten you?"

Why ask: everyone with real mileage has scars and opinions. It's also a great closer — candidates relax, and relaxed candidates tell you true things.

Answer guideline — any real answer works; common honest ones

  • Error-handling boilerplate (if err != nil fatigue) — bonus if they can also defend it.
  • The typed-nil interface trap (Part 1, Q4), zero values silently hiding missing data, no real enums / iota awkwardness.
  • Ecosystem scars: module migration pain, GOPATH era, a specific library that burned them, generics arriving late or being clunky at the edges.
  • The shape matters more than the item: a specific story + how they adapted = experience. Generic complaints they read on Reddit = exposure.

Strong signals

  • A specific pain with a specific story, told with some affection
  • Can argue the other side (why Go chose this trade-off)
  • The complaint is consistent with the experience they claimed in Q19

Weak / red flags

  • "Nothing really, Go is great" — shallow exposure or fear of honesty
  • Complaints that reveal misunderstanding ("no generics" in 2026, "you can't test in Go")
  • Pure language-war talking points with no personal story attached

3/5 = one honest specific gripe. 5/5 = gripe + story + steelmans the design choice. "Nothing" is a 1 — treat it seriously.

The 45-minute playbook

MinutesSegmentWhat you're doing
0–5IntroYou, the role, the format. Tell them it's fine to say "I don't know" — you'll get cleaner signal all session.
5–15CalibrationQ19 + Q20. Their answers tell you which technical questions to pick and how hard to probe.

Choosing questions on the fly: if Q19 revealed heavy-traffic experience, lean into Part 2 and Q14 (pools, races, leaks). If they came from a small startup owning everything, lean into Part 3 (structure, shutdown, retries). Match the questions to the experience they claim — that's how you verify the claim.

Cross-cutting red flags

  • Syntax without semantics: can't explain slice/map behavior beyond syntax — mostly CRUD glue, thin Go mileage.
  • Theoretical concurrency: textbook answers in Part 2 but no personal story of a race, leak, or deadlock. For a 3–4 year Go backend dev that's a strong signal of shallow exposure.
  • No context anywhere: if it never appears while discussing HTTP services, cancellation, or timeouts — it's ubiquitous in real Go codebases; its absence is loud.
  • Everything outsourced: deploys, migrations, debugging, CI all "handled by the senior / the DevOps team." Once is fine; a pattern means they've been a passenger.
  • Stories that shrink under follow-ups. Real experiences gain detail when probed; invented ones lose it.

Green flags worth over-weighting

  • Says "I don't know" cleanly, then reasons toward an answer out loud.
  • Trade-off language unprompted: "it depends on write frequency", "simpler but slower", "we chose X because Y".
  • Corrects themselves mid-answer — self-monitoring beats fluent wrongness.
  • War stories where they were wrong first and describe how they found out.

Deciding

  • Score each asked question 1–5 on the scorecard during the interview — memory reconstruction an hour later is where bias creeps in.
  • For a 3–4 year Go backend role: hire signal = averaging 3.5+ with at least one 5 in Part 2, and no red-flag pattern. No-hire = any two cross-cutting red flags, or concurrency averaging under 3 — you can't backfill concurrency judgment on the job as easily as, say, CI habits.
  • Write your verdict + three supporting observations immediately after the call, before Slack distracts you. If you can't produce three observations, you didn't probe enough — fix that next screen, not the candidate's score.

Reference: the printable scorecard has every question with a 1–5 rubric on one page — print it or keep it open per candidate.
