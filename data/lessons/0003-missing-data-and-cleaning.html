Missing data & cleaning

If Lesson 2's practice isn't all green yet, no problem — today reuses the same file and the same two coerced columns, so finishing this one also finishes that one. Today's actual question: once you know what's missing, what do you do about it — and why "just fill with 0" is usually the wrong instinct.

Recap, in one line

Lesson 2 turned silent problems into visible ones: pd.to_numeric(..., errors="coerce") and pd.to_datetime(..., errors="coerce") convert what they can and turn the rest into NaN / NaT instead of crashing. That's detection. Today is the decision that comes after: drop it, fill it, or flag it — and each is a different claim about what the missing value means.

1 · Three moves, and their SQL cousins

MovepandasSQL cousin
Dropdf.dropna(subset=["col"])WHERE col IS NOT NULL
Filldf["col"].fillna(value)COALESCE(col, value)
Flagdf["col_missing"] = df["col"].isna()An extra computed boolean column

All three are one-liners; none of them is the automatically-correct one. The question that picks between them is always the same: what does "missing" mean for this specific column, in this specific analysis? A missing order_date and a missing discount_code are not the same kind of absence, and shouldn't get the same treatment by reflex.

2 · Why "just fill with 0" is usually wrong

Filling a numeric gap with 0 is tempting because it's fast and keeps every downstream .sum()/.mean() from choking on NaN. The problem: 0 is not "we don't know" — it's a specific, real claim ("this order was worth nothing"), and it silently becomes indistinguishable from a genuine zero-value row that really did mean zero. Two consequences that bite in practice:

  • Averages lie. amount.mean() over five real orders plus one fake 0 pulls the average down — you've quietly told every downstream consumer "orders are cheaper than they are," and nothing in the output flags that one row was fabricated.
  • "Missing" and "actually zero" become the same bucket forever. Once filled, there is no query that can tell you which rows were originally unknown — that information is destroyed, not just hidden.
✗ Fill and forget
df["amount"] = df["amount"].fillna(0)
# now indistinguishable from a REAL $0 order — mean() is quietly wrong
✓ Fill AND flag
df["amount_was_missing"] = df["amount"].isna()
df["amount"] = df["amount"].fillna(df["amount"].median())
# median is a defensible stand-in AND you can still ask "which rows were fabricated?"

The reasoning that picks a fill value: a median is usually a safer default fill than the mean, because one extreme outlier barely moves it — a $50,000 typo in an otherwise $20–200 order column would drag the mean far from reality, but leaves the median almost untouched.

3 · When to drop instead

dropna is the right call when the column is essential to the row's meaning and no fill value is honest — a missing order_id, or a missing order_date in an analysis specifically about ordering timing. Dropping is a decision with a cost, though: you're discarding whatever else that row knew. Always check how much you're actually throwing away before doing it:

df["order_date"].isna().sum()             # how many rows would this drop?
df = df.dropna(subset=["order_date"])    # SQL: DELETE FROM ... WHERE order_date IS NULL,
                                          # but on a copy — the original df is untouched unless reassigned

Q: "A numeric column has some missing values. What's your default move?" A strong answer, out loud: "It depends what the column means, so first I check how much is missing and whether it's essential to the row. If it's essential and there's no honest fill value, I drop those rows — after checking how many that actually is. If a fill is defensible, I use the median over the mean, since it resists outliers, and I ALSO add a boolean flag column so 'originally missing' isn't silently indistinguishable from a real value later. Filling with a bare 0 without a flag is the mistake I'm avoiding — it quietly corrupts every average downstream." The subtext: missing data is a design decision with a paper trail, not a call to fillna(0) and move on.
